Flood Preparedness & Safety Guide

Floods are among the most common and destructive natural disasters in the Philippines. Caused by excessive rainfall, poor drainage, or coastal surges, they can damage property, infrastructure, and endanger lives. Being informed is key to reducing risks.

A flood is a significant rise in water levels in rivers, lakes, or coastal areas that overflows and submerges normally dry land. It may result from heavy rains, dam failure, or storm surges.

  • Natural: Typhoons, thunderstorms, high tides, tsunamis, earthquakes.
  • Human-made: Deforestation, improper waste disposal, construction near waterways, dam failures.
  • Minor Flooding: Shallow flooding in low-lying areas.
  • Major Flooding: Widespread, deep flooding with strong currents.
  • Flash Floods: Sudden, intense floods in mountainous or urban areas.

Before the Flood

  • Know if your area is flood-prone and identify evacuation routes.
  • Prepare a go-bag with food, water, flashlight, radio, batteries, medicines, and important documents.
  • Keep electronics and valuables on high shelves.
  • Have a family communication and evacuation plan.
  • Follow local weather updates from PAGASA and LGUs.

During the Flood

  • Evacuate early when advised; don’t wait until water rises.
  • Never walk or drive through floodwaters — even shallow currents can be dangerous.
  • Turn off electricity and gas lines if possible.
  • Move to the highest point in your home or seek shelter.
  • Use text or radio for updates; avoid spreading misinformation.

After the Flood

  • Return home only when authorities declare it safe.
  • Avoid contact with floodwater — it may be contaminated.
  • Boil or purify water before drinking.
  • Check for structural damage before entering homes.
  • Report damaged electric or water lines immediately.
  • Clean and disinfect items affected by floodwater.
  • Plant trees and protect forests.
  • Keep canals and waterways clear.
  • Avoid illegal construction near rivers.
  • Report illegal logging and dumping.
Source: PAGASA - Learning Tools on Floods
